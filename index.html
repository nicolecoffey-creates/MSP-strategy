MSPs & IT Services · Growth-Stage Niche
Seed small. Earn the transition.
Most brokers ignore MSPs until they're large enough to be worth quoting. This model does the opposite: build relationships early, right-size the strategy at every stage, and be the broker who was already there when the MSP outgrows the PEO model.
Why this model works in this industry
🤝
MSPs are loyal to vendors who speak their language
The MSP community is tight, channel-driven, and heavily referral-based. An owner who hears you talk about MRR, per-seat economics, and the difference between a help desk tech and a vCIO will remember you. One who hears generic broker language will not. Loyalty earned early compounds fast in a community this well-networked.
📈
The PEO exit is a predictable life event, not a cold pitch
PEOs make sense under 20 employees. Above 25, the per-employee economics start inverting, and by 35–50 employees, most MSP owners are paying for services they no longer need bundled together. The broker who planted the seed owns the conversation when that inflection point arrives, rather than cold-calling in from the outside.
💬
The referral flywheel is faster here than almost anywhere
MSP owners talk to each other constantly, in peer groups, in vendor ecosystems, in Slack communities, at ASCII events. A broker who helps one owner make a smart transition gets mentioned by name in those rooms. One good case study inside a peer group can generate more qualified introductions than a year of outbound.
📦
The PEO referral fee funds the early relationship
Placing a small MSP into the right PEO isn't charity work. Most PEO relationships generate an ongoing referral commission to the placing broker. The early accounts pay while the book matures, and the transition revenue is additive when growth arrives. The model has positive unit economics at both stages.

Traditional group health broker relationship with a consultative layer: plan strategy, renewal analysis, benchmark data, and continued positioning as the expert who has been with them since the early days.
Why now, in San Diego specifically
📍
San Diego has a dense and growing MSP market
San Diego's defense, biotech, and professional services base creates a strong MSP demand environment. Many of these MSPs serve regulated verticals (CMMC compliance for defense contractors, HIPAA for biotech) where benefits sophistication also signals professional credibility to clients, not just employees. That's a two-sided value proposition most brokers never identify.
ChannelCon 2026 is in San Diego, August 3–5. This is the flagship conference for the Global Technology Industry Association, the major vendor-neutral MSP membership org. Having this land locally means the highest-value national MSP conference is a 20-minute drive away. That is a rare and time-sensitive access point.
The honest challenges
Volume required
Small MSPs are small accounts. The book needs volume to generate meaningful revenue during the early-stage phase. Systemizing the onboarding process and managing a larger number of relationships simultaneously is a prerequisite for the model to work.
⚠ Churn and PE Risk: How to Mitigate It
MSP consolidation by private equity is accelerating fast. A client acquired by a PE-backed roll-up may not survive as an independent long enough to reach Stage 4. The mitigation strategy is deliberate vertical targeting: prioritize MSPs serving industries where PE roll-ups are structurally less common.
Lower PE consolidation risk
✓
Defense / CMMC compliance MSPs
Federal contract compliance requirements make these MSPs difficult to bundle into a roll-up. Sticky, mission-critical contracts. San Diego's defense base makes this a natural local priority.
✓
Healthcare IT / HIPAA-focused MSPs
Complex compliance requirements and liability exposure make consolidation harder. Owners in this vertical tend to want to retain control and stay independent longer.
✓
Legal / financial services MSPs
Client confidentiality requirements, bar association rules, and FINRA/SEC oversight create barriers to PE roll-up. Clients won't follow a consolidated provider.
✓
Government / public sector IT MSPs
Contract vehicles (GSA schedules, SEWP), clearance requirements, and federal procurement rules create strong barriers to acquisition and consolidation.
Higher PE consolidation risk
✗
Generalist SMB MSPs
No vertical lock-in, commoditized service, easy to bolt into a roll-up platform. The most targeted segment by PE consolidators.
✗
Break-fix / low-MRR shops
Easy acquisition targets for roll-ups buying client bases at low multiples. Often sold or absorbed before reaching the 25-employee transition threshold.
✗
MSPs in hot consolidation markets
Markets like general cloud services and co-managed IT are PE-active right now. Avoid over-indexing here for Stage 1 relationship building.
Practical filter: When qualifying a small MSP for Stage 1, ask two questions early: "What verticals do most of your clients come from?" and "Is staying independent something you've thought about long-term?" Those two answers will tell you more about retention risk than any other signal.

What the PEO audit conversation looks like
How to open the door with a small MSP today
Ask
"Do you know what your all-in cost per employee is through your PEO, including admin fees, not just the benefits line?"
Offer
"I'll run an audit at no cost. It'll take 20 minutes of your time. If the numbers look fine, you'll know you're in good shape. If they don't, you'll know what to do about it when the time comes."
Frame
Be explicit: "I'm not here to move you off your PEO today. At your size, it may still be the right call. But I specialize in IT firms specifically, and I want to make sure you have the right setup for where you're headed."

The inflection point: when the PEO math stops working
This is the segment where the real broker value lives. The PEO that made sense at 10 employees is now a significant annual overpayment, and the owner usually knows it but hasn't had anyone put numbers in front of them.

The inflection point: PEO pricing is typically structured as a percentage of payroll or a flat per-employee-per-month fee. At 10 employees the bundle is worth it. At 30, the MSP is paying for HR compliance infrastructure they could replicate with a standalone HRIS at a fraction of the cost, while getting a far more customizable, competitive benefits package through direct broker placement.
Buyer persona
CEO
MSP Owner / CEO (growth stage)
Has been running the MSP for 5–8 years. 20–35 employees, steady MRR, a small leadership team. Probably runs EOS or something similar. Starting to recruit at the vCIO and senior engineer level, where benefit quality is actually discussed in offer conversations. Knows the PEO is expensive but hasn't had a clear alternative presented.
"We're paying Insperity $180 per person per month and I genuinely don't know what I'm getting for that anymore. Someone told me we should probably look at moving off it but I don't know who to call."
Pain points
•PEO cost is opaque and increasing with every hire
•Plan design is rigid, locked to what the PEO offers
•Can't customize benefits to compete for senior technical hires
•No one explaining what the transition would actually involve
•Worried the exit will be operationally painful
Decision triggers
→Losing a senior engineer hire to a competitor citing better benefits
→CFO or ops manager flags the PEO line item in an annual review
→Another MSP owner in their peer group mentions they transitioned off
→Planning a hire at vCIO or practice lead level for the first time
What the transition involves
A well-managed PEO exit is a 60–90 day project
Step 1
Full cost audit
Itemize the actual all-in PEO cost: admin fees, benefits premiums, payroll processing, workers comp markup. Most owners have never seen this number clearly.
Step 2
Side-by-side comparison
Build a direct benefits scenario using a group health strategy. Show the plan design options, employee cost share, and employer cost per employee. Let the numbers make the case.
Step 3
Transition plan
Map the HRIS, payroll, and compliance stack needed to replace the PEO bundle. Identify the right timing, usually aligned to benefits renewal and ideally January 1 for a clean calendar-year start.
Step 4
Execute and onboard
Handle the PEO exit notice, stand up the group health plan, coordinate the HRIS transition, and communicate the change to employees. The broker owns this process end to end.
Competitive landscape at this stage
high threat
The PEO itself
PEOs are not passive when an exit conversation starts. Many will restructure pricing, offer concessions, or assign a dedicated account rep. The broker needs to show that the delta even after PEO concessions still favors a direct strategy, and that flexibility is a permanent feature of the transition, not a one-time deal.
medium threat
Generalist brokers who stumble into the conversation
A P&C broker who holds the MSP's cyber or E&O policy may see the renewal conversation as a benefits cross-sell opportunity. They won't have MSP-specific knowledge but they have an existing relationship. Differentiating on niche expertise wins this matchup consistently.
low threat
PEO brokers / consultants
Consultants who specialize in PEO placement are not incentivized to move clients off PEOs. They may offer a PEO-to-PEO transition but rarely a PEO-to-direct one. Not a meaningful competitive threat in the transition segment.
White space
No broker is actively positioned as the MSP PEO-exit specialist. The transition itself is well-documented and predictable, but most MSP owners at this stage are either waiting for someone to bring it to them or dealing with the friction of figuring it out alone. A broker who shows up already knowing the process, already speaking the MSP's financial language, and already trusted by the owner earns this engagement with almost no competition.

California clients only California-specific compliance layer
⚖️
CA PEO registration requirement
California requires PEOs to register with the Employment Development Department (EDD) as a licensed employer. Not all nationally operating PEOs are properly structured for California co-employment. An MSP owner who doesn't know this may be in a PEO arrangement that creates unintended compliance and tax liability. Surfacing this in the audit conversation immediately demonstrates CA-specific expertise.
🔁
Cal-COBRA: continuation coverage for groups of 2–19
Federal COBRA only applies to employers with 20 or more employees. California's own continuation coverage law, Cal-COBRA, applies to groups of 2–19. This matters directly for the 20–35 employee transition segment: when an MSP exits a PEO near that threshold, continuation coverage obligations are different than they would be in a non-mandate state. Getting this wrong during a PEO exit creates real employee-facing exposure. This is a CA-only consideration.
🏥
Covered California for Small Business (CCSB)
California operates its own small group exchange available to employers with 1–100 employees. CCSB is a legitimate option alongside the fully insured commercial market for MSPs coming off a PEO at 25–35 employees. Most generalist brokers don't walk CA clients through this comparison. A broker who does is providing something genuinely different. This is a CA-only option.
📋
California individual mandate
California reinstated its own individual mandate after the federal penalty went to zero. Employees are required to maintain coverage or pay a state tax penalty. This affects how employees evaluate plan options and what employers need to communicate during open enrollment. MSP owners who've been in a PEO haven't had to manage this communication layer directly. This is a CA-only compliance requirement.
👷
AB 5 contractor classification risk
California's AB 5 is one of the most aggressive contractor classification laws in the country. Many MSPs use 1099 contractors for overflow helpdesk work or project-based engineering. If those workers are misclassified under CA law, they may have benefits entitlements that create exposure. Surfacing this during a PEO audit conversation adds immediate, concrete value well beyond the benefits question. This is a CA-only legal issue. Do not raise with out-of-state clients.
🏦
San Diego carrier market
The dominant carriers in San Diego are regional: Kaiser Permanente, Sharp Health Plan, Blue Shield of California, and Anthem Blue Cross CA. Network adequacy, cost structure, and plan design vary significantly from national defaults. A broker quoting a San Diego MSP needs to be working with the right carriers for this specific market, not defaulting to what they'd quote in Arizona or Nevada. Carrier market is San Diego and Southern CA specific.
How to use this layer: The CA compliance items above are entry-point conversation starters, not just disclosures. Each one represents something a CA-based MSP owner is exposed to that their current generalist broker almost certainly has not surfaced. Leading with even one of these in an audit conversation signals expertise that has nothing to do with quoting.

MSP Terminology Reference
Speak their language from day one
MSP owners have a distinct vocabulary built around how they price, operate, and grow their businesses. Using the right terms signals that you understand their world. Using the wrong ones signals that you don't. This glossary covers the terms that come up most in MSP business conversations, organized by category.
Business model and financials
MRR
Monthly Recurring Revenue
The primary health metric of an MSP's business. Total contracted monthly revenue from managed services agreements. When an MSP owner says "we're at $200K MRR," they mean $200,000 in monthly recurring contract revenue. This is how they think about business size, not headcount or annual revenue.
ARR
Annual Recurring Revenue
MRR multiplied by 12. Used when discussing valuations, growth trajectory, or comparing to industry benchmarks. A $200K MRR MSP has $2.4M ARR. PE buyers and acquirers typically value MSPs as a multiple of ARR.
Per-seat / per-device
Pricing model
How most MSPs price their services. Per-seat means charging a fixed monthly amount per employee (or user). Per-device means charging per managed endpoint (computer, server, firewall). An MSP quoting "$85 per seat per month" is charging $85 per employee per month for their managed services. This is directly analogous to how a broker should frame benefits costs to an MSP owner.
Break-fix
Legacy pricing model
The old way of doing IT: a client calls when something breaks and pays an hourly rate to fix it. No recurring contract, no predictable revenue. Most modern MSPs started in break-fix and transitioned to recurring managed services. An MSP still doing significant break-fix work is earlier-stage and less financially stable.
Churn
Client attrition rate
The percentage of MRR lost each month from clients who cancel or reduce their contracts. High churn is the primary threat to MSP valuations. A low-churn MSP (under 2% monthly) is far more valuable and stable than a high-growth MSP losing 5% of revenue per month. Asking about churn tells you a lot about the health of the business.
Utilization rate
Technician efficiency
The percentage of a technician's working hours that are billable or applied to managed services contracts. An MSP targeting 75-80% utilization means they want technicians spending that share of their time on client work. Low utilization means overstaffed or undercontracted. High utilization means the team is stretched and retention pressure is higher.
QBR
Quarterly Business Review
A quarterly meeting between the MSP and each of its clients to review performance, discuss upcoming needs, and strengthen the relationship. QBRs are how MSPs demonstrate value and protect against churn. A broker who frames their own annual renewal process as a QBR-style conversation will immediately earn credibility.
Tools and infrastructure
PSA
Professional Services Automation
The operational backbone of an MSP. Handles ticketing, time tracking, invoicing, project management, and client communication in one platform. The dominant PSAs are ConnectWise Manage, Autotask (Kaseya), and HaloPSA. Asking which PSA an MSP uses tells you a lot about how sophisticated their operations are.
RMM
Remote Monitoring and Management
The tool MSPs use to monitor, manage, and deploy software to client devices remotely without physically being on-site. The major RMM platforms are NinjaOne, ConnectWise Automate, Kaseya VSA, and Datto RMM. An MSP without a strong RMM is not operating at scale. These platforms also run the vendor partner programs that are key access channels.
Stack
Technology stack
The collection of tools and platforms an MSP uses to deliver its services: PSA, RMM, security tools, backup, documentation platform, and so on. MSPs are deeply attached to their stack choices. Vendors who integrate with or complement the stack get more traction than those who don't.
NOC
Network Operations Center
A team (internal or outsourced) that monitors client networks and responds to alerts around the clock. Larger MSPs run their own NOC; smaller ones outsource it. An MSP with a NOC is operating at a higher service tier and typically has more complex client relationships and higher per-seat pricing.
SOC
Security Operations Center
Like a NOC but focused specifically on cybersecurity monitoring, threat detection, and incident response. MSPs that run or outsource a SOC are operating as MSSPs (Managed Security Service Providers) and typically serve clients in regulated verticals. These firms tend to be more sophisticated, better-resourced, and more stable.
MSSP
Managed Security Service Provider
An MSP that specializes in cybersecurity services: threat monitoring, incident response, compliance management, and security tooling. MSSPs are typically more profitable than generalist MSPs and serve larger clients. The talent they're recruiting (security engineers, threat analysts) is among the most competitive in the IT labor market.
Roles and titles
vCIO
Virtual Chief Information Officer
A senior strategic role at an MSP, typically the person who serves as a fractional CIO for the MSP's clients, helping them plan their technology roadmap, budget for IT, and align tech decisions with business goals. vCIOs are among the highest-compensated staff at an MSP (typically $120–200K+) and are the hardest to recruit. When an MSP starts hiring at the vCIO level, that is a strong signal they're ready for a more sophisticated benefits conversation.
Level 1 / L1 tech
Entry-level helpdesk
Entry-level technicians who handle basic helpdesk requests: password resets, software installs, printer issues. Typically $45–60K. High turnover. These are the employees most affected by the benefits gap between a PEO master plan and a customized direct strategy.
Level 2 / L2 tech
Mid-level engineer
More experienced technicians who handle escalated tickets, server administration, networking, and more complex troubleshooting. Typically $65–95K. The core of most MSP delivery teams and the most important retention target for a growing MSP.
Service manager / dispatcher
Operations role
The person who routes incoming tickets to the right technician, manages SLAs, and keeps the service board moving. Often the first non-technical hire an MSP makes. In smaller shops, the owner does this themselves. This person is often the de facto HR contact and may be the one who actually manages the PEO relationship.
Community, channel, and culture
The channel
The MSP ecosystem
When MSPs say "the channel," they mean the entire ecosystem of MSPs, vendors, distributors, and service providers that make up the IT services industry. Being "in the channel" means you operate within and understand this ecosystem. Vendors who sell to MSPs are "channel companies." A broker who wants to reach MSPs needs to become a known quantity in the channel.
Peer group
Curated owner community
Small, curated groups of non-competing MSP owners (typically 10–15) who meet quarterly to share financials, operational data, and business strategies. IT Nation Evolve (formerly HTG) is the most prominent peer group program. These are the highest-trust environments in the channel. A recommendation from inside a peer group is the most powerful introduction a vendor can get.
EOS
Entrepreneurial Operating System
A business management framework from Gino Wickman's book "Traction" that is almost universally adopted across the MSP industry. If an MSP owner mentions "rocks," "the L10," "the IDS," "Level 10 meetings," or "Visionary/Integrator," they're running EOS. Knowing what EOS is signals immediately that you've spent time around MSP owners.
MSP 501
Annual industry ranking
The annual ranking of the top 501 MSPs globally, published by Channel Futures / MSP Summit. Being on the MSP 501 is a significant credibility marker within the community. Owners track it. If a prospect mentions they've been ranked, they're proud of it. Referencing the MSP 501 in conversation signals you know the industry.
Co-managed IT
Hybrid service model
A service model where the MSP works alongside a client's existing internal IT team rather than replacing it. The MSP handles overflow, specialized tasks, or 24/7 coverage. Co-managed IT tends to serve mid-market clients (100–500 employees) and represents a growing revenue segment for MSPs scaling beyond the SMB market.
CMMC
Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification
A Department of Defense cybersecurity certification framework required for MSPs and IT firms serving defense contractors. CMMC-certified MSPs have cleared a rigorous compliance process and are typically more operationally mature, better-documented, and less likely to be rolled up by a generalist PE firm. In San Diego's defense-heavy market, CMMC is a key qualifier.
SLA
Service Level Agreement
Contractual commitments to clients about response times, resolution times, and uptime. SLAs are what MSPs are held accountable to. A missed SLA costs the MSP money (via contract penalties) or clients (via churn). High SLA pressure on the team = high stress = retention risk = benefits matter more.
How to use this in a conversation
Phrases that earn credibility immediately
Instead of saying
"How many employees do you have?"
Say this
"What's your current headcount, and are most of them technicians or do you have a mix of vCIOs and engineers on the team?"
Instead of saying
"How much revenue does your company make?"
Say this
"What does your MRR look like right now, and has growth been pretty steady over the last couple of years?"
Instead of saying
"We can help you get better benefits for less money."
Say this
"Most MSPs at your stage are paying their PEO somewhere between $150 and $200 per seat per month all-in. I'd like to show you what that number actually breaks down to and what you could do differently."
Instead of saying
"Are you happy with your current HR setup?"
Say this
"Are you still on a PEO, or have you moved to a direct setup? And when you're recruiting at the L2 and vCIO level, are benefits coming up in those conversations?"
